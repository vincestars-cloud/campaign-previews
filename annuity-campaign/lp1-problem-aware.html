THE HIDDEN RISK

Your Retirement Calculator Is Hiding Something From You

Most retirement calculators assume average market returns. They show you a smooth, upward line. But the market does not move in smooth, upward lines. It crashes. It recovers. It crashes again.

The problem is not whether the market will recover. It always does. The problem is whether it will recover before you need the money.

Financial planners call this "sequence-of-returns risk." In plain English: a market crash in the first few years of your retirement can permanently destroy your portfolio -- even if the market recovers later. Because you are withdrawing money while it is down.

Your calculator does not show you this scenario. And it is the most common reason retirees run out of money.

SCENARIO A: LUCKY TIMING

Market goes up first, then crashes

$800K portfolio, withdrawing $4,000/month

Money lasts 32 years

SCENARIO B: UNLUCKY TIMING

Market crashes first, then recovers

Same $800K portfolio, same $4,000/month withdrawals

Money runs out in 18 years

THE DIFFERENCE

Same returns. Same withdrawals. Same person.

The only difference? When the crash happens. And you cannot control that.

THE 3 TRAPS

Three Things You Have Probably Already Tried (And Why They Did Not Work)

Trap #1: The Commission-Based Advisor

You sat down with someone from Edward Jones, Northwestern Mutual, or a similar firm. They ran your numbers through their software, recommended a portfolio, and told you everything would be fine. But something felt off.

What they did not tell you: Their recommendations are influenced by what pays them the highest commission. The products they suggest may be perfectly fine -- or they may be unnecessarily expensive versions of simpler alternatives. You have no way to know, because they are not required to disclose their compensation on every recommendation.

The guide explains the difference between commission-based advisors and fee-only fiduciaries -- and why it matters for your specific situation.

Trap #2: DIY Investing and the Panic Sell

Maybe you decided to manage your own money. Vanguard index funds, a 60/40 split, rebalance once a year. Smart, disciplined, low-cost. You did everything right -- until the market dropped 30% and you sold at the bottom.

Why this happens: When it is hypothetical money, you are rational. When it is the money that determines whether you eat or go back to work at 72, the reptilian brain takes over. The average investor underperforms the S&P 500 by nearly 4% annually because of behavioral mistakes -- buying high, selling low, and panicking at exactly the wrong moment.

The guide shows you how to remove the temptation to panic-sell by taking your essential expenses off the table entirely.

Trap #3: The Cash and CD Safety Illusion

When the market terrifies you, cash feels safe. CDs feel safe. Sitting on the sidelines feels safe. And for a year or two, it is. But then you notice that groceries cost 15% more. Healthcare premiums went up. Gas is higher. Your safe money is buying less every single month.

The invisible thief: At 4% inflation, your purchasing power is cut in half in 18 years. A retiree at 65 with $1M in cash will have the equivalent of $500K in buying power by 83. You avoided the market crash but lost the same amount of money -- just slowly enough that you did not notice until it was too late.

The guide explains how guaranteed income can include inflation-protection features that cash and CDs simply cannot match.
