Email 2 of 6Day 5Education
Why bleach does not actually fix mold
It is the most common mistake homeowners make — and it actually makes things worse inside the wall...

Hey [First Name],

Quick one today — I want to share something I see homeowners try almost every week before they call me.

They find what looks like mold. They grab a bottle of bleach. They scrub it off. The surface looks clean. They feel better.

And two months later, it is back. Worse than before.

Here is what is actually happening.

Bleach is mostly water. Water absorbs into porous materials — drywall, wood, grout. The chlorine evaporates off the surface almost immediately. What is left behind? Moisture. Deep inside the wall.

Mold does not live on the surface. It lives in the material itself. When you hit it with bleach, you kill the surface layer. But you have just fed the root system with water. It comes back from the inside, often spreading further than the original spot.

DIY solutions do not fail because you did it wrong. They fail because the problem is structural — and you cannot clean your way to structural.

The same is true for vinegar, hydrogen peroxide, and most over-the-counter products. They work on non-porous surfaces. They do not work on the materials that make up your home.

This is why I always say: before you spend money on anything, know what you are actually dealing with. An inspection is not an expense — it is the thing that keeps you from spending $8,000 solving the wrong problem.

When you are ready to know what is actually in your home, I am here.

Email 4 of 6Day 15Conflict of Interest / Positioning
What most people forget to ask a mold company
Five questions every homeowner should ask before hiring anyone. Most companies will fail at least one...

Hey [First Name],

Before you hire any mold company — including me — you should ask these five questions. If a company will not answer them directly, walk away.

  1. “Do you also do remediation?”
    This is the most important one. A company that both inspects and remediates has a financial incentive to find mold and recommend more than necessary. That is simple economics. Keep inspection and remediation separate.
  2. “Will your report include air sampling results, not just visual findings?”
    Visual inspections alone miss a lot. Lab-analyzed air samples tell you what is actually circulating in your home.
  3. “Can I see a sample report?”
    Professional inspection reports are detailed, readable, and actionable. If they cannot show you a sample, that is a red flag.
  4. “Are you certified, and by which organization?”
    Look for NORMI, IICRC, or ACAC certification. Uncertified companies operate with zero accountability.
  5. “What happens if you find nothing?”
    The right answer: “You get a clean report and peace of mind.” Any hesitation here tells you everything.
“You cannot have the same company that inspects also do the remediation — that is a conflict of interest.” — Real customer, before they found us.

For what it is worth: we pass all five. We inspect. We do not remediate. That is the whole point of what we do.

Want a printable version of this checklist? Reply and I will send it over.

The allergy-vs-mold confusion (most doctors get this wrong)
Allergy season is here. So is mold season. Most people cannot tell them apart — but one clear sign separates them...

Hey [First Name],

It is that time of year — pollen counts are up, everyone is congested, and half the people I talk to are convinced they are just having a bad allergy season.

Some of them are right. Some of them are wrong in a way that matters a lot.

Here is the one test that separates allergies from mold exposure:

Leave your home for 48–72 hours. A weekend trip. Staying with family. A hotel. Anywhere else.

If your symptoms improve significantly while you are away and return within a day of being home — you do not have seasonal allergies. You have a home problem.

The difference is: allergy symptoms improve when you leave the house. Mold symptoms follow you — but are worst when you are home.

Seasonal allergies respond to antihistamines. They improve outdoors on low-pollen days. They do not disappear during a road trip and reappear the moment you walk back through your front door.

I see this misdiagnosis constantly — and not just by homeowners. Most primary care physicians do not ask the travel question. They see congestion and fatigue and reach for the allergy pad.

One person put it exactly: “I kept thinking it was allergies. Turns out it was the HVAC the whole time.”

If the travel test rings a bell for your family, it is worth knowing for certain. That is what I am here for.
