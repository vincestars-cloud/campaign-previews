Strategy Comparison

4 Ways Federal Employees Bridge the FERS Income Gap

Each strategy uses your TSP differently. The right choice depends on your creditable service years, your high-3 average salary, your risk tolerance, and how close you are to your Minimum Retirement Age.

1

Stay in TSP & Withdraw

G Fund Safety / L Fund Glide Path

Keep your entire balance inside the Thrift Savings Plan after separation. Use monthly withdrawals from the G Fund for stability, or let the L Income Fund gradually shift from C/S/I growth funds into bonds as you age. TSP's 0.055% expense ratio is the lowest in the industry — roughly 40x cheaper than the average retail mutual fund.

  • Lowest fees in any retirement plan (0.055%)
  • G Fund backed by U.S. government securities
  • L Funds auto-rebalance based on target date
  • Partial withdrawals available post-separation
  • Limited to 5 TSP fund choices (C, S, I, F, G)
  • No guaranteed lifetime income stream
  • Market downturns can erode withdrawals
  • Withdrawal flexibility still trails IRAs
Best for: Federal employees comfortable with market exposure who want the lowest-cost option and don't need guaranteed income beyond their FERS annuity and Social Security.
2

TSP Rollover to IRA

Full Investment Flexibility

Roll your TSP balance into a Traditional or Roth IRA after separation from federal service. This unlocks thousands of investment options — individual stocks, bonds, REITs, ETFs, and managed portfolios. You gain full control over your withdrawal strategy and can implement Roth conversion ladders to manage your tax bracket through retirement.

  • Thousands of investment choices (vs. TSP's 5)
  • Roth conversion ladder for tax optimization
  • Beneficiary flexibility for estate planning
  • Consolidate old 401(k)s and IRAs in one place
  • Higher fees (0.20%–1.0%+ vs. TSP's 0.055%)
  • Requires active management or advisor
  • Lose access to the G Fund's unique guarantee
  • No guaranteed income without purchasing annuity
Best for: Employees with $500K+ TSP balances who want sophisticated tax planning, Roth conversions, or need to consolidate multiple retirement accounts.
3

TSP Annuity Purchase

Guaranteed Lifetime Income

Use part or all of your TSP balance to purchase a life annuity through MetLife (TSP's contracted provider). This converts a lump sum into a guaranteed monthly payment for life — similar to how your FERS annuity works. Options include single life, joint life with your spouse, and increasing payment riders to keep pace with inflation.

  • Guaranteed monthly income for life
  • Eliminates longevity risk completely
  • Joint-life option protects surviving spouse
  • No investment decisions required
  • Irrevocable — cannot access principal once purchased
  • Rates fluctuate; bad timing locks in low payments
  • No inflation protection unless you pay for the rider
  • If you die early, remaining value may be lost
Best for: Risk-averse employees who prioritize income certainty above all else and want to replicate the guaranteed nature of their FERS annuity.
4

The Income Floor Strategy

Partial TSP + Fixed Annuity + Growth

Split your TSP into two buckets: roll enough into a fixed index annuity (outside TSP) to cover the gap between your FERS annuity + Social Security and your essential monthly expenses. Keep the rest invested in TSP's C and S Funds for long-term growth. Your essential expenses are 100% covered by guaranteed income. Your discretionary spending is funded by market growth.

  • Essential expenses covered by guaranteed income
  • Remaining TSP grows tax-deferred in equity funds
  • Market crashes don't threaten your bill payments
  • Psychological peace — you can weather any downturn
  • Requires careful calculation of "floor" amount
  • Annuity portion is less liquid than TSP
  • More complex than a single-strategy approach
  • Need to select the right annuity product
Best for: Federal employees 3–7 years from retirement who want the security of guaranteed income for essentials while still participating in market growth with their remaining TSP balance.

Critical Timeline

The FERS Supplement: Your Bridge Income (and Its Cliff)

If you retire before 62 with at least 20 years of creditable service, OPM pays the FERS Supplement — a temporary benefit that approximates your Social Security. But it stops abruptly at 62. Here's the timeline every federal employee must understand.

56–57
MRA Retirement
You separate from federal service at your Minimum Retirement Age (56–57 for most FERS employees born after 1970). Your FERS annuity begins immediately with 30+ years of creditable service.
FERS annuity: $2,851/mo
57–62
FERS Supplement Period
OPM pays the FERS Supplement to bridge the gap until Social Security eligibility. It approximates what your Social Security benefit would be, prorated for your years of FERS service. It is subject to an earnings test if you work.
Supplement: ~$1,200/mo
62
The Supplement Cliff
The FERS Supplement stops the month you turn 62 — regardless of whether you claim Social Security. If you delay SS to get a higher benefit, you face a gap of zero bridge income. This cliff can mean losing $1,000–$1,500/month overnight.
Supplement drops to $0
62–70
Social Security Decision
You can claim SS as early as 62 (reduced 30%) or delay to 70 (increased 24% above full retirement age). The Income Floor strategy uses the fixed annuity to bridge this gap — so you can delay SS and get a permanently higher payment.
SS at 67: $2,400/mo

The Supplement Cliff is the #1 planning blind spot for FERS employees. Most federal retirement calculators show your income with the Supplement included — but don't clearly show what happens the month it disappears. If your only plan is "claim Social Security at 62," you're locking in a permanently reduced benefit (up to 30% less than your full retirement age amount). The Blueprint shows you exactly how to bridge the 62–67 gap without being forced into early SS claiming.

Roth conversions are one of the most powerful tax optimization strategies for federal employees — but timing is everything. The ideal window is the gap years between your separation from federal service and when Social Security and Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) begin. During these years, your taxable income drops significantly, which means you can convert Traditional TSP dollars to Roth IRA dollars at a much lower tax rate.

For example: a GS-13 retiring at 60 might go from a 22% marginal bracket while working to a 12% bracket in retirement (before SS and RMDs). Converting $40K–$50K per year during this window builds a tax-free Roth pool that won't increase your future IRMAA Medicare premiums, won't be subject to RMDs, and passes to your beneficiaries tax-free.

The Blueprint includes a year-by-year Roth conversion roadmap with the exact bracket thresholds for 2026 and the IRMAA tipping points to avoid.

The FERS survivor benefit is a critical factor that many employees overlook. If you elect the full survivor benefit, your surviving spouse receives 50% of your unreduced annuity — but your own annuity is reduced by 10% while you're alive. If you elect the partial survivor benefit (25% to spouse), your reduction is 5%.

This matters for strategy selection because the survivor benefit only applies to your FERS annuity — not your TSP, not Social Security (which has its own survivor rules), and not a private annuity. If you purchase a joint-life annuity as part of the Income Floor strategy, your spouse continues to receive that income regardless of the FERS survivor benefit election.

The Blueprint walks through three scenarios: single retiree, married with full survivor benefit, and married with partial survivor benefit. Each scenario changes the optimal Income Floor split.

If you've already separated from federal service and your TSP balance is sitting in the G Fund or L Income Fund, the strategies in this Blueprint still apply. You can still roll TSP dollars into an IRA or purchase an annuity at any point after separation. The key decisions — how much to keep in TSP vs. roll over, whether to purchase guaranteed income, and when to claim Social Security — are the same whether you retired last month or five years ago.

That said, if you're already drawing Social Security and have begun RMDs, the Roth conversion window may be smaller or closed. The Blueprint will still help you optimize your TSP withdrawal strategy and evaluate whether an income floor makes sense for your remaining balance.

The TSP annuity (purchased through MetLife) and a private fixed index annuity serve similar purposes — both convert a lump sum into guaranteed lifetime income — but they differ in important ways:

TSP Annuity (MetLife): Simple, low-cost, purchased directly through TSP.gov. Limited options (single life, joint life, with/without cash refund). Rates fluctuate monthly and cannot be locked in advance. Once purchased, it's irrevocable. No index-linked growth potential.

Private Fixed Index Annuity: More complex, but offers potential for higher payouts through index-linked crediting methods. Can include income riders with guaranteed withdrawal percentages. May offer liquidity features (10% free withdrawal). Requires rolling TSP into an IRA first. Higher internal costs (built into the product design, not charged directly as fees).

The Blueprint includes a direct comparison of both options with current rate examples so you can see the actual dollar difference for your TSP balance.

This Blueprint is specifically designed for FERS employees. CSRS employees have a fundamentally different retirement structure — your annuity computation uses a higher multiplier (1.5%–2.0% per year vs. FERS' 1.0%–1.1%), you generally don't receive Social Security from your federal service, and you don't receive the FERS Supplement.

That said, the TSP strategy concepts (Stay in TSP vs. Rollover vs. Annuity vs. Income Floor) still apply to CSRS employees with TSP balances. However, the math examples, FERS-specific computations, and Social Security timing analysis would not be applicable to your situation.
